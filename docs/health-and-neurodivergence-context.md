## Physical Health And Work Location Context

James also reports insulin-dependent Type 2 diabetes, advanced peripheral neuropathy, Charcot foot, a collapsed diaphragm, gastroparesis, tinnitus, frozen shoulders on both sides, and colon cancer in remission.

General clinical framing:
- Insulin-dependent Type 2 diabetes can require glucose checks, medication, food timing, restroom access, and treatment flexibility.
- Peripheral neuropathy is nerve damage associated with diabetes. It commonly affects the legs and feet and can involve pain, numbness, tingling, burning, weakness, balance issues, or reduced tolerance for sustained walking or standing.
- Charcot foot is a serious diabetes-related foot complication that can make avoidable walking, standing, stairs, and foot stress materially risky.
- A collapsed or paralyzed diaphragm can reduce breathing capacity because the diaphragm is central to normal breathing. It can cause shortness of breath, especially with exertion or when lying down, and can make physically demanding movement harder.
- Gastroparesis is delayed stomach emptying and can create unpredictable digestion, nausea, food-timing issues, restroom needs, and blood-sugar instability.
- Tinnitus can create constant internal sound such as ringing or buzzing, making noisy or audio-heavy environments more difficult.
- Frozen shoulder, also called adhesive capsulitis, can cause pain and reduced shoulder range of motion; bilateral frozen shoulders can make reaching, lifting, carrying, and some workstation setups harder.
- Colon cancer in remission may still require surveillance appointments, medical follow-up, privacy-respecting flexibility, and support for lingering or changing side effects.

Work-relevant interpretation for James:
- Remote work is generally preferred because it reduces avoidable physical load, commuting strain, sensory load, and unnecessary movement.
- Hybrid work can be workable if the onsite expectations are predictable, limited, and worth the physical cost.
- Fully onsite jobs are higher-friction, especially if they require commuting, long walking distances, frequent movement between locations, prolonged standing, crowded/noisy spaces, or constant live interaction.
- Rigid schedules are higher-friction when they prevent glucose management, meals, medication, restroom access, medical appointments, or symptom-management breaks.
- These conditions do not mean James cannot work. They mean job design, location expectations, schedule structure, and remote/hybrid flexibility materially affect fit.
- Relocation or frequent onsite attendance should be treated as a real cost, not a minor preference.
